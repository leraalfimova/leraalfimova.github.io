Museums of the U.S.

The story shows how many museums per capita America has, which of them gather most reviews and what disciplines are the most common.

It’s easy to assume that all art in America is concentrated in New York City and other territories could be deemed not worthwhile. The easiest way to investigate whether this statement is true or not is to calculate how many museums per capita in each state there are.

We extracted some data from the Museum Universe Data file that shows all museums of the States - including such disciplines as historical, geographical, children’s museums, etc.

It turned out that the state of New York is the richest state for museums per capita, but Virginia, Alabama and Massachusetts aren’t far behind. Also, some of midwestern states fare well, but a likely explanation is population, which is much lower than the top states.

Source: Institute of Museum and Library Services, U.S. Census Bureau

The next question is what kind of museums are prevalent. It seems obvious that the number of art museums would be higher than others, but according to the data it’s not. Historical societies and general museums which hold variety of multidisciplinary collections are well ahead of art. It’s important to notice that general museums apparently include collections of art works, but the data has difficulty categorizing them because of different fields the objects on display can belong to. Historical museums rank next. There are a tiny number of children’s museums, comparatively.

Source: Institute of Museum and Library Services

TripAdvisor has data on 1,600 U.S. museums - the same data was used for creating the app MUSEO. On TripAdvisor, you can find out not only information about reviews, but also recommended length of visit, fees and so on. The feedback champion is The National 9/11 Memorial & Museum. It’s not a big surprise: the memorials is free and located in a busy part of Manhattan. The Metropolitan Museum of Art -- the world famous Met -- goes next.
